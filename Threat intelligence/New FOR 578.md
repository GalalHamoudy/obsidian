What defines a capable CTI analyst? A CTI analyst can:
+ Fully analyze successful and unsuccessful intrusions by targeted threat actors
+ Construct descriptions of campaigns, actors, and organizations
+ Seek out, collect, and properly exploit intelligence from others
+ Generate intelligence from their own data sources and share it accordingly
+ Manage intelligence to further the objectives of their organizations

Classic Intelligence Sources :
Classic intelligence has abbreviations associated with the sources of intelligence. Generally, these can be broken out by collection and analysis (or derivative intelligence), using human and technological means. Common acronyms:
+ HUMINT: Human intelligence collection (interpersonal).
+ GEOINT: Geospatial intelligence collection (from satellites).
+ MASINT: Measurement and signature intelligence (from radar signatures, nuclear detonation signatures, and so on).
+ OSINT: Open-source intelligence collection (from libraries, public records, or the internet).
+ SIGINT: Intelligence derived from signal intercepts (cell phone communications or tapping of communication lines)


Example Types of Analysis : 
Data-Driven Analysis
+ Requires good datasets and straightforward problems
+ Accuracy is based on the dataset's accuracy and completeness
+ Logically-driven and easily replicated by other analysts observing it

Data-driven analysis is enabled by pre-existing or previously developed analytic models and are based on the assumptions that both the data and the model are accurate and applicable. To borrow an example from an unrelated field—virologists model the anticipated spread of diseases by using data collected on the virus itself and how it replicates and transmits, and applying that information to models that have been built over time, including agent-based models and growth-matrix models. The reliability of these models rely on using accurate data and applying the right models. Intelligence analysts often use a similar approach to things such as assessing military capabilities and troop movements, or the stability of a new government in a developing nation.

Conceptually-Driven Analysis:
- Numerous unknowns and undefined variables and relationships
- Often immediate interpretation of complex concepts
- Accuracy is driven by mental models and feedback over time

Conceptually-driven analysis is used much more frequently in intelligence analysis due to the simple fact that there is rarely enough accurate data to precisely model a situation—though that will change as the field continues to professionalize. Often, analytic problems have an undetermined number of variables and it is not always apparent which variables are dependent variables or independent variables, nor even if a variable is relevant. In these situations, the individual analyst often determines which mental model or theory is most applicable and will work within that model. Conceptually driven analysis does not often lead to analytic consensus, and during this type of analysis, you will often hear the analytic mainstay “It depends.” This is not to say that conceptually driven analysis is not valid—often, this is the world of uncertainty where analytic judgment is most needed. When leveraging conceptually driven analysis, it is especially important to identify biases that may influence intelligence analysis and leverage structured analytic techniques that we will cover later in this section

**Working Memory**: The primary classifications of memory are short-term, long-term, and working memory. All three play a role in analysis; however, working memory is especially critical. Working memory is the system that processes inputs and determines whether or not they are important, encoding them either for long-term memory storage or short-term memory. Working memory supports analysis by telling your brain what to focus on and retain and is core to the next tool in our analytic box, pattern analysis.

**Pattern Recognition**: This is a cognitive process that matches information from external sources to information that is stored in long-term memory. There are six primary theories of pattern analysis; however, the ones most applicable to intelligence analysis are template matching, prototype matching, and top-down processing.

**Template matching theory** states that every object or experience is processed by the brain and stored as a template in long-term memory. When presented with a situation, long-term memory is searched for a matching template to explain the situation. It is important to remember that our brains are not computers, and template matching is not like string matching in programming. How we interpret something and what we perceive about it also gets stored in the template. The experience-based templates we store are useful, but they are rarely objective representations.

**Prototype matching** is similar to template matching; however, it does not search for an exact match of a template, but to an ”average” of similar templates referred to as a “prototype.” This type of pattern matching is more flexible and supports pattern matching with incomplete information, which is a useful skill in intelligence analysis.

**Top-down pattern matching** uses previous knowledge to “fill in the gaps” when we do not have complete information. This is one of the reasons context is so important; it enables that previous knowledge to be applied more accurately. When learning a new language, it is far easier to understand a previously unknown word when it is presented in a sentence rather than on its own. Top-down pattern matching is not always accurate; it also accounts for optical illusions and a large number of experience-related cognitive biases.
